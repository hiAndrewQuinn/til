[noslopgrenade.com](https://noslopgrenade.com/) is a website
in the ilk of
[nohello.com](https://www.nohello.com/),
exhorting the reader not to just copy and paste
giant walls of text into the chat. One line stood
out as curious to me:

>Nobody writes essays in Slack. It's only possible because of AI copy-paste.

This has not really been my experience, but it depends
on where you draw the line at "essays". For me a 3-paragraph
reply, of about 2-3 sentences apiece, is where I draw the
line between "This can be a Slack message" and 
"This is better as an email". But the examples given in
No Slop Grenade suggest even that might be too much for
the author's liking.

I do this because I like a communication style where I lay out
all the facts, and - crucially - *give the other party time*
to get back to me. In fact when I make that transition from
Slack message to email it usually comes with the proviso that
I don't actually expect to see a reply to the email unless I
follow it up a few days later, with a shorter, punchier
question. THis is all intentional and similar to
[Postel's law](https://lawsofsoftwareengineering.com/laws/postels-law/)
but for human communication.

There is a better reason to adopt the laconic-by-default style
of No Slop Grenade: Job security. A memo generally doesn't
just reveal the conclusions the author wrote, but the
reasoning process by which they got there. If you assume that
every email you ever send, every Slack message and Teams
transcript, every Confluence page, etc. you send in a corporate
environment are ultimately going to be surfaced, read, and
reinterpreted many more times by LLMs in the future rather
than flesh and blood human beings, then it's easy to see how
leaving a consistent, detailed track of your thinking makes
it easier for your coworkers to ask e.g. 
"What would be Andrew Quinn's modal take on X?",
*without them needing you to give it in the first place*.
The more often you do this, the more often that answer will
be in the ballpark of correct. If the AI can give you-like
answers 90% of the time, and your job is 90% answers,
you might well find yourself being cut down to ~20% time.

This may be at odds with your career goals. If you hide the
byproduct and only ever give short, oracular answers to what
you are being asked, you thin this pool out considerably. Nor
do you have to be a jerk about it - you can always draft your
own "thinking..." message, then just keep it somewhere else
until and unless someone else responds `"Ok. Why"`. My hunch
is that, in most institutions, this question will rarely be
asked, and you will continue to extract a quite nice rent
on the ideas which seem to exist within only your head.

Modulo all this by the local conditions of your own
employment, and by your own sense of ethics. 
If you are a heavily-paid consultant charging
hundreds per hour, this might actually be a fantastic idea.
If you work in a place without at-will employment laws, you
might also find it worthwhile: It's hard to argue that someone
should be fired because
[Rovo agent](https://support.atlassian.com/rovo/docs/agents/)
can already answer all the questions they would be asked
on the strength of their own stellar internal documentation.
If you are a crypto-communist, it's probably a very good
idea to let the silicon beast convert your labor into capital
even more than it already does, etc. etc.
